Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) is a progressive condition in which the kidneys lose their ability to filter waste and excess fluids from the blood. This can lead to dangerous levels of fluid, electrolytes and waste in the body.

When kidney function declines to a certain point, dialysis or a kidney transplant becomes necessary for survival. Early detection and proper management can help slow disease progression and prevent complications.

The disease often develops gradually and may not produce noticeable symptoms until significant damage has occurred. This makes regular screenings important for those at risk.

Types of Kidney Disease

  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD): Long-term loss of kidney function, often caused by diabetes or high blood pressure.
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): Sudden decline in kidney function, often due to dehydration, infection, or medications.
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD): A genetic disorder that causes fluid-filled cysts to grow in the kidneys.

How Kidneys Work

Your kidneys filter waste and excess fluids from your blood, help regulate blood pressure, produce hormones, and maintain electrolyte balance.

Symptoms of Kidney Disease

Kidney disease often develops without noticeable symptoms. As it progresses, you may experience:

  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Swelling in the legs, ankles, or feet (edema)
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Decreased appetite
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Changes in urination (frequency, color, or foaming)
  • Shortness of breath
  • High blood pressure that is difficult to control

Diagnosis

To diagnose kidney disease, healthcare providers use:

Blood Tests:

  • Serum Creatinine: Measures waste levels in the blood.
  • Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate (eGFR): Indicates how well the kidneys are filtering.

Urine Tests:

  • Urinalysis: Detects protein, blood, and other abnormalities.
  • Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio (ACR): Measures protein leakage in urine.

Imaging & Biopsy:

  • Ultrasound or CT scans to view kidney structure.
  • Kidney biopsy may be used to determine the cause of kidney damage.

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on the stage and cause of kidney disease.

Lifestyle Changes:

  • Low-sodium, kidney-friendly diet
  • Regular physical activity
  • Quitting smoking
  • Controlling blood sugar and blood pressure

Medications:

  • Blood pressure medications (e.g., ACE inhibitors, ARBs)
  • Diuretics for swelling
  • Medications to control cholesterol or anemia

  • Kidney Transplant: A surgical procedure to replace a damaged kidney with a healthy one from a donor.

Diet & Lifestyle

Managing your diet is essential to slow kidney disease progression.

Kidney-Friendly Diet Tips:

  • Limit sodium (salt) to control blood pressure
  • Watch potassium and phosphorus intake
  • Choose lean proteins in moderation
  • Stay hydrated, but avoid excessive fluids if advised

Healthy Habits:

  • Exercise regularly
  • Avoid NSAIDs and over-the-counter medications without doctorâ€™s advice
  • Manage stress with relaxation techniques

Prevention

You can reduce your risk of kidney disease by:

  • Controlling high blood pressure and diabetes
  • Avoiding excessive use of painkillers
  • Staying hydrated
  • Eating a balanced, low-sodium diet
  • Quitting smoking
  • Getting regular check-ups, especially if you have risk factors

Living with Kidney Disease

Kidney disease can affect your lifestyle, but with the right care, many people lead full, active lives.

Coping Strategies:

  • Join a support group
  • Work with a renal dietitian
  • Stay informed about your condition
  • Plan ahead for treatments like dialysis or transplant if needed

Practical Tips:

  • Communicate openly with your healthcare team
  • Monitor symptoms and blood pressure at home
  • Keep a list of medications and medical appointments
